# Survey Papers (how to use them)
> Survey (review) papers map an entire subfield — its taxonomy, key methods, open problems, and the
> citation web — in one document. The fastest way to *enter* a new area: instead of reading 50 papers
> blind, read one good survey to get the map, then deep-read the 3–5 papers it tells you matter.

**Why it matters:** when you're ramping on an unfamiliar area (for a project or an interview topic),
a survey gives you the vocabulary, the taxonomy, and the canonical references in a day. Knowing how
to *use* surveys (and their limits — they age fast and reflect the authors' framing) is a core
"learn a new field quickly" skill.

**⭐ Start here — suggested path:**

1. **Find a recent, well-cited survey** — search arXiv / [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/) for "survey" or "review" + your topic, sorted by citations and recency. *A current, highly-cited survey is the best single entry point to a field.*
2. **Read the taxonomy first** — read the survey's introduction + its taxonomy figure/table, not the whole thing. *The taxonomy *is* the map: it tells you how the field is organized and where each method fits.*
3. **Mine the citation list** — pull the 3–5 papers the survey calls foundational or SOTA and deep-read those (using card 01). *Surveys are a curated reading list; the real understanding comes from the primary papers they point to.*
4. **Read the "open problems" section** — it tells you where the frontier is and what's unsolved. *This is gold for interviews ("what are the open challenges in X?") and for picking what to learn next.*
5. **Mind the expiry date** — check the survey's date; in fast areas (LLMs, agents) a 2-year-old survey is partly stale. *Pair an older, deep survey with a recent newsletter/leaderboard for the latest.*

## 📄 Key Papers
- [Deep Learning (review)](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature14539) — **LeCun, Bengio & Hinton (2015, Nature)** — the canonical field-defining review; a model of what a great survey does.
- [Representation Learning: A Review and New Perspectives](https://arxiv.org/abs/1206.5538) — **Bengio, Courville & Vincent (2013)** — a classic survey whose taxonomy still organizes the field.
- [A Survey of Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.18223) — **Zhao et al. (2023)** — a comprehensive, heavily-cited LLM survey; the template for entering a fast-moving area.
- [Retrieval-Augmented Generation for LLMs: A Survey](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.10997) — **Gao et al. (2023)** — example of using a survey to map a subfield's methods and open problems.

## 📰 Articles / Blogs (free, no paywall)
- [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/) — **Google** — sort by citations + "review articles" to find the highest-signal survey for a topic.
- [Connected Papers](https://www.connectedpapers.com/) — **Connected Papers** — a visual alternative to a survey: build the citation graph yourself.
- [Lil'Log](https://lilianweng.github.io/) — **Lilian Weng** — long-form posts that function as living surveys of fast-moving subfields.
- [The Gradient](https://thegradient.pub/) — **The Gradient** — editorial overviews that survey directions and debates in the field.

## 📚 Books (free, with chapters)
- [Dive into Deep Learning](https://d2l.ai/) — **Zhang et al.** — each chapter is a mini-survey-with-code of a topic; a structured alternative to scattered surveys.
- [Understanding Deep Learning](https://udlbook.github.io/udlbook/) — **Simon Prince** — a free textbook that consolidates many surveys into a coherent map.
