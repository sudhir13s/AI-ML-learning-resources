# Data Visualization Basics — Matplotlib · Seaborn
> You can't understand data you can't see. **Matplotlib** is the foundational plotting library (full
> control, a bit verbose); **Seaborn** sits on top of it for beautiful statistical plots in one line.
> Together they power **exploratory data analysis (EDA)** — the step where histograms, scatter plots,
> and correlation heatmaps reveal what's actually in your data before you model it.

**Why it matters:** EDA is the first thing you do on any dataset, and visualization is how you do it —
spotting skew, outliers, class imbalance, and relationships between features. Take-homes are routinely
judged on whether your plots show you *understood* the data, not just that you fit a model.

**⭐ Start here — suggested path:**

1. **Learn the Matplotlib mental model** — read [Matplotlib Quick Start](https://matplotlib.org/stable/users/explain/quick_start.html). *Figure vs Axes — the one concept that makes Matplotlib click.*
2. **Watch a complete tour** — [Matplotlib Crash Course](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3Xc3CA655Y4). *Line, bar, scatter, histogram — the everyday plots in one go.*
3. **Add Seaborn for fast statistical plots** — read [Seaborn: An introduction](https://seaborn.pydata.org/tutorial/introduction.html), then watch [Seaborn Full Course](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6GUZXDef2U0). *One-line histograms, boxplots, and heatmaps for EDA.*
4. **Practice on real data** — do [Kaggle Learn: Data Visualization](https://www.kaggle.com/learn/data-visualization). *Builds the habit of plotting before modeling.*
5. **Go deeper on Matplotlib patterns** — [Corey Schafer: Matplotlib (Part 1)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UO98lJQ3QGI). *Careful, well-structured series for real fluency.*
